Part One





There is a part of me that feels embarrassed, ashamed even, that I love her so much. She is only one person, one heartbeat among millions.  And yet I know the exact rhythm of it. I tell myself I don’t need her, that I can breathe on my own, that I was whole before she ever existed. None of it matters. Every breath she takes is sacred. I listen for it without realizing I’m listening. Her pulse doesn’t just steady me, it sets the pace for everything around me. When it slows, the world becomes serene; when it quickens, life becomes sharper. 
And it always finds its way back to me. 
I make sure of that.
 Mila

November 22nd

Mila hurried through the cobblestone streets of Stillwater, her satchel bouncing lightly against her hip. The air smelled of river mist, baked bread, and faint smoke from the hearths tucked into crooked cottages. Lanterns swayed, painting the stones gold, while fairies zipped past, wings sparkling in her peripheral vision.
She barely noticed them. Her mind was on getting home before the sun disappeared completely. Ellis was waiting.
She had gone out for herbs after discovering her garden had come up short again. There was something wrong with the soil here. No matter what she planted, nothing grew quite the way it should.
The leaves emerged smaller than normal, roots stayed shallow, and even mint, which had a habit of taking over everything it touched, seemed reluctant to spread. 
If she were being honest, it embarrassed her. Still, something about this place made her stay anyway.
She had come to Stillwater on pure rage.  The cottage was supposed to be hers, the last real piece of her grandmother she could hold onto, and finding out it had ended up in the hands of a vampire was more than she could take.
So she had come ready to fight for it.
But he did not fight her.
There had been barely any resistance at all. Like he had been expecting her, like the outcome had already been decided long before she arrived. He didn’t take any money. Only a small favor. When she agreed, he handed the cottage over. Just like that.
She still did not understand why.
Only that everything inside was still there. Her grandmother’s trinkets, supplies, and grimoires untouched, the cottage itself holding onto magic in its walls like it was waiting for her to learn how to listen.
At first, the thought of settling in a town filled with vampires and werewolves had made her uneasy.
She expected people to watch her, to ask questions, and to wonder why a witch had moved into the old cottage on the edge of town.
Instead, no one seemed to care.
As the weeks passed, she spent her days sorting through her grandmother’s belongings, cleaning the cottage room by room, and trying to bring the neglected garden back to life. The townspeople hardly gave her a second glance. No one asked where she came from. No one asked why she’d returned. Most people seemed too busy with their own lives to notice hers.
She blended into Stillwater more easily than she ever expected.
She could wander the crooked streets with dirt on her hands and river mist caught in her hair, and no one paid much attention. She was just another face passing through town.
A prickle crept along her spine.
Mila slowed.
Except to Brax. She could always feel him before she saw him. 
“What is it?” she asked, voice steady, though her hand tightened on her satchel strap.
Brax stepped from the shadowed alley, dark eyes glinting like obsidian. “You’re getting better at sensing me.”
She tilted her head. “Or you’re getting less careful.”
He gave a faint shrug. “I’m worried about you.”
“You don’t need to be.”
“You know that won’t stop me.” His gaze lingered, sharp and intense. He moved closer, just enough that she could smell him. The air cooled as he approached, and memories began to flicker. She recalled the time he waited for her outside the market in the rain, and to the hand he offered when no one else had cared. Something tugged at her heart, but she ignore it. 
“I have to get home,” she said.
“Ellis?”
She exhaled, half amused, half frustrated. “Yes. And he’s waiting.”
Brax’s jaw flexed, but he didn’t argue. “Be careful.”
“I always am.”
He held her gaze a moment longer, then bowed his head slightly and stepped back into the shadows. The alley swallowed him.
Mila stood there for a few seconds before turning toward home, trying not to think about the way he had looked at her.
* * *
Inside her small kitchen, Mila leaned over a wooden counter, chopping herbs. The knife moved in a steady rhythm, but she wasn’t really paying attention. Ellis was behind her, close enough that she could feel the heat radiating from him. 
“You’re quiet,” she pointed out.
He hummed, a low, vibrating sound she felt in her chest. “Just watching you,” he said.
She glanced up, meeting his eyes. They were steady and bright. He was looking at her the way he always did, as if she were the only thing in the world that mattered. For a moment, she thought she could see something more, something raw and wild. 
“You’re warm,” she said, pressing her hand to his chest. His heartbeat thudded slow and strong.
“Aren’t I always?” He laughed.
“I suppose you are,” Mila said.
Outside, the wind shifted from the north, brushing through the open window. Leaves rustled. The scent of wet earth came with it. Ellis’s ears twitched just slightly, a reflex she knew she wasn’t supposed to notice. He turned his head towards the window.
“Maybe we should do the ritual tomorrow,” Mila said softly. “Would you be ready for that?”
Ellis brought his attention back to her. The slight fear in his eyes vanished when he looked at her. “I think that’s a great idea.”
They moved effortlessly together through her kitchen. He spun her gently, careful not to step on her toes. She laughed, a little breathless, and he smiled down at her. 
When they stilled, she rose onto her toes and looped her arms around his neck. Even then, her chin barely brushed his collarbone.  “Promise me something,” she whispered.
He brushed her hair from her face, thumb lingering, warm and strong. “Anything.”
“Promise me you’ll stay.”
“I’m right here,” he said. 
The kitchen was warm and golden. The herbs smelled sharp and fresh. Outside, the town carried on unaware that inside, everything was perfect.
 Ester
November 23rd

Ester’s room was nearly empty. A basket of belongings sat in the corner, rarely disturbed, as if even she had no reason to sift through them. Beyond that, there was a wardrobe holding a few plain black dresses, a clock that ticked far too loudly, and a bed where she now sat atop an old, tattered blanket.
She looked down at her hands. Her skin was pale enough that the veins beneath looked like faded ink. She turned them over and flexed her fingers. 
The more time she spent with her roommate and coworker Willow, the more disoriented she felt.
Willow moved through the world so effortlessly. She laughed loudly, spoke freely, and filled every room she entered with warmth. Being near her stirred something uncomfortable in Ester, a quiet ache she couldn’t name. It felt like standing outside a brightly lit window on a winter night, watching something she could see but never quite reach.
Before coming to Stillwater, Ester had never thought much about happiness. She had simply existed from one day to the next. Now, Willow’s laughter lingered in her mind long after it faded. The brush of her hand, even by accident, left Ester strangely unsettled.
Ester rose from the bed, stiff and precise, and made her way downstairs. She was not meant to go into work until lunch, but she felt something unsettled in the air below and wanted to see what was happening.
In the kitchen, she found Dani pacing across the floor, brows drawn tight. Her copper hair matched the pots simmering on the stove.
“What’s going on?” Ester asked, scanning the room for the source of the tension. 
Dani spun to face her. “We open in ten minutes, and Willow hasn’t come back with the potatoes.”
“When did she leave?”
“An hour ago.”
“That doesn’t seem like her,” Ester muttered, glancing toward the door.
“Mrs. Wimble will be awake soon, and if she sees I haven’t started breakfast, it’ll be my head on a platter!” Dani’s voice rose before she caught herself, lowering it quickly. Yelling would only wake Mrs. Wimble faster.
Dani had a talent for making every problem personal. As if the rest of them would somehow escape Mrs. Wimble’s temper. Dani claimed she was Mrs. Wimble’s favorite, though there had never been any proof.
“Do you want me to go find Willow?” Ester asked.
Dani squinted at her, clearly surprised by the offer. “I mean, sure, I guess. My best guess is there was a potato famine overnight, and she’s wandering the markets in despair.”
“Maybe she’s hurt,” Ester said quietly.
“Hurt? Why would you even suggest that?”
Ester let out an even breath, calming her anger. “It’s possible.”
“Well then, go,” Dani said, waving her toward the door.
Ester turned and stepped out into the streets of Stillwater.
The morning air was fresh, sunlight scattering across last night’s rain still gathered in shallow pools. It must have rained while she was asleep. Strange. She usually felt it before it came, the shift in pressure, the heaviness in the air.
She had not left this early in a long time.
From here, she could see a few shops that would usually sell potatoes, but Willow was nowhere. Where would she have gone? The city smelled of wet stone and soot from distant chimneys. 
Ester kept to the edges of the street, where the buildings cast long, protective shadow. Her cloak helped dull the brightness, but it did not erase it. When sunlight spilled into her path, she crossed it quickly.
Every sound reached her: a shutter banging, a cart rattling in the distance, but none of it belonged to Willow. Her mind ticked over possibilities, each more worrying than the last.
Ester’s eyes caught a narrow street. A door swung slightly on its hinges. She remembered glimpsing someone there before, always in Willow’s company. A pretty girl with dark hair and large eyes. She hadn’t known the girl’s name. She hadn’t paid attention. But now something drew her forward, a pull she couldn’t explain, a whisper of curiosity mingled with dread.
She slipped into the alley and crouched behind a wall. Inside, she saw the girl arranging small jars and candles on a counter. Another figure, a boy she didn’t recognize but could easily tell was a werewolf, shifted from one foot to the other, hands brushing the edge of the table.
“You’ll be safer if you stay,” the girl said. 
“No, Mila,” he said, low, hesitant. “I don’t think I will be.” He stepped back.
Mila moved her hands over the counter again, adjusting the jars. “It’ll be easier this way. We’ve talked about it. You agreed.”
Ester stayed unseen. She didn’t move closer, just watched. The candles and jars seemed ordinary enough, yet the care with which they were handled made the room feel controlled. Whatever they were discussing felt as though it was life or death.
The boy glanced toward the door again. 
“Ellis!” The girl snapped her fingers, drawing attention back to her. 
He froze, dark eyes flashing. “I can’t.”
“You’re running again,” Mila said. “You’re weak.”
Her words struck him like a blade. His jaw tightened, and suddenly he barked, “I’m not weak!”
In the same motion, his shape twisted. His muscles bunched and stretched, and his nails lengthened. Fur sprouted along his arms and spine. His jaw elongated, teeth sharpening, fangs glinting in the light. Ester pressed herself into the shadows. Ester pressed herself into the shadows. She had never seen a werewolf change before, and she knew what they were capable of when they did.
Her chest tightened.
This wasn’t supposed to happen in Stillwater.
Not after the elixir. Not after all these years.
And yet… here he was.
A knot formed in her stomach.
If she ignored it, someone could get hurt. If she reported it, she’d be dragging herself into a headache of paperwork, questions, and people.
Neither outcome felt like a win.
Before Ester could think about this further, Ellis lunged, slamming Mila against the wall, and pinning her there with one large hand. The jars on the counter rattled.
Ester gasped and clamped her hand over her mouth. Should she call for help? Should she run? Her instincts screamed to do both, yet she couldn’t tear her eyes away.
“You’re not strong,” Mila said, voice level, unshaken. “You only think you are.”
She didn’t thrash or try to get away, she just stared at him, feet floating above the floorboards.
Ellis pressed harder, claws brushing the counter. Ester felt the raw, unpredictable danger in him. Then, slowly, almost reluctantly, he released her. He sniffed the air, nostrils flaring, and turned toward the window. “Someone is here,” he growled.
Ester didn’t hesitate.
She was already moving before the thought fully formed, slipping back into the street and breaking into a run. Whatever happened in there was not worth staying for.
“Ester?” Willow stood a few paces away beneath the apothecary’s awning, brow furrowed. Her pink curls were a mess from the wind, soft and uneven around her face. “What are you doing out here? You look like you’ve seen a ghost.”
Not a ghost, she wanted to say. Something worse.
Instead, she said, “I was looking for you.”
Willow stepped closer, studying her face. “For what?”
Ester couldn’t help but notice every detail. Willow’s cream dress, loose and slightly wrinkled at the sleeves. A thin chain at her neck. Small bracelets that moved when she did.
“For Dani. She needs the potatoes.” 
Willow’s eyes squinted.
That was when she noticed the bag in Willow’s hand.
It was definitely too small to be potatoes.
Willow followed her gaze and, without thinking, drew the bag closer to her body.
Ester noticed that too.
Ester listened to Willow’s heartbeat. Why was it so quick?
“Dani said your shift started,” Ester said.
“I guess I lost track of time.”
Willow wasn’t evasive by nature. This was strange.
Ester watched her for a moment longer than she meant to.
“Are you okay?” Willow asked.
Ester gave a small scoff, but it didn’t land the way she wanted it to. “Am I okay? You’re the one missing your shift.”
Willow blinked, a little confused. “You just don’t look well, is all. Is something going on?”
Ester hated when Willow asked what was wrong. She could lie to most people without thinking. With Willow, it fell apart every time. Something about the way she spoke, soft and steady, made everything Ester kept buried start pushing up on its own.
The fact that she didn’t really belong anywhere in Stillwater, not even with the other vampires. The way everyone looked at her like she didn’t quite fit what she was supposed to be. The fact that her family was dead. All of them. Leaving her with no one and nothing that felt like home.
And then there was Willow.
Being near her didn’t fix anything. It made things worse in a way Ester couldn’t easily explain. Like something she kept reaching for even though she knew it threw her off balance.
She should have put space between them a long time ago.
Ester swallowed, forcing her voice to stay steady. “I’m fine.”
It didn’t sound true.
“I know you’re lying to me,” Willow said.
“So are you,” Ester said, gesturing towards Willow’s bag. “Why were you late for work?”
Then her hand tightened around the bag at her side. “Right,” she said softly. “Okay.”
A pause stretched between them.
Then something in Willow’s posture changed. “Alright then. I’ll see you around.”
With that, she turned and began fluttering towards the boarding house. 
“Willow,” Ester called far too late. 
She was glad it didn’t reach her because she didn’t know what she would’ve said if she turned around.
Ester stayed where she was.
She looked down at the rainwater pooled at her feet. It caught the light easily, bright and clean from above. But underneath, there was mud settled at the bottom, flecks of ash, scraps of whatever the night had washed in.
It looked clean until you looked too long.
She exhaled slowly.
 Willow
November 24th

Willow had almost convinced herself that Stillwater was ordinary.
From her second-story window at the boarding house, the city looked the way a city ought to. Narrow lanes bustled with people, merchants called out from their stalls, and townsfolk brushed past each other as they moved to and fro. A fairy hovered near The Square, her bow dancing across strings while coins clinked at her feet.
A sharp crack snapped against the glass.
The sound vibrated through the pane and into her fingertips where they rested on the sill. Another pebble struck, harder this time.
“Oh, Brax,” she breathed.
She wrapped her fingers around the latch and tugged hard. It resisted at first, but she pressed her thumb into it causing it to pop open. The window swung outward, letting in the cool night air.
When he saw her, he looked relieved to see she was home. His hood was up despite the fading sun and his dark eyes glinted with barely contained anger. 
“Can I come up?”
Willow glanced behind her. The Kitchen was closed for the night, and Mrs. Wimble would either be knitting by the fire or already asleep. She waved him over sharply.
With a grace no other creature could have matched, Brax scaled the stone wall and slipped through the window without a sound.
“What’s wrong?” Willow asked.
“That idiot broke up with Mila.”
The words tumbled out of him, sharp and furious. His eyes burned brighter. Willow felt it then, his unmistakable aura. She was trying to focus on what he was saying, but she felt as though she were back in time. The feeling was both comforting and heart wrenching. 
“How dare he break her heart,” Brax went on. 
Willow reached for him without thinking, resting her hand against his cheek. He stopped mid-breath.
His skin was ice cold, but familiar. “Shh,” she said quietly. “Sit down.”
Brax huffed and dropped into a chair, forcing himself still.
Willow pulled another chair close and sat across from him. “They fight all the time,” she said carefully. “I’m sure it’s no big deal.”
“That’s where you’re wrong. The dog left town.”
“Left?” Willow repeated. “Left Stillwater?”
He nodded. “Gone. Packed up and vanished after their fight.”
People did not leave Stillwater. Not once they stayed long enough to stop pretending they were only passing through. “Is she okay?”
“Is she okay? She’s Mila. She’d never admit she wasn’t okay,” he said with a sigh. “She won’t even let me see her.” A look of realization swept over his face. “What if he hurt her? That’s why she won’t let me in.”
“Brax,” Willow said sternly. “You’re getting carried away. Let me talk to her, okay? I’ll handle this.”
He nodded, some of the fire draining from his posture. “Meet me at The Raven later?”
“Later,” she agreed. “And be quiet when you leave. Miss Wimble will have my head if she knew a boy was in my room.”
A faint smile tugged at his mouth before he slipped back out the window and disappeared into the night.
Willow closed the glass and leaned against it, exhaling slowly. A curl slipped loose from its tie and brushed her cheek. 
Willow surveyed her room, wondering if there was anything she could bring to Mila’s that might help. The space was modest, crowded with her belongings yet carefully arranged. The order might have seemed invisible to anyone else, but she knew exactly where everything lived.
Books rose in neat towers from the floor with glistening titles embedded on the spines that read: The Life of Trees, The Magic of One’s Spirit, Enchanting Bread Recipes, and dozens more. A small, delicate chair carved from the same pale wood as her wardrobe sat near the window. Soft pink curtains brushed the sill, perfectly matched to the quilt spread across her bed.
Ribbons trailed from dresser knobs. Bracelets rested in shallow dishes. A shawl hung loosely over the back of her chair as though it had just exhaled and settled there. Greenery filled the space, she placed a spell of soft magical lights tucked among the leaves, a little bit of sun glowing just enough to keep the plants thriving, even in the windowless corners.
Deciding she’d better just go, she headed for her door. 
Mila and Ellis had always seemed like a strange pair to Willow. Mila was strong, but she also carried a deep emotional awareness, an understanding that came naturally to her. Ellis, on the other hand, only seemed to remember or care about what was directly in front of him. Keeping his attention was a challenge at best.
Maybe it wasn’t his personality that drew Mila in. Willow wasn’t blind, she could see it too. He was ridiculously good-looking: tall, broad, with a face so symmetrical it looked carved from stone. His brown eyes looked serious, even though the rest of him was immature and careless.
On her way downstairs, Willow caught a glimpse of Ester through the narrow crack of her door. Her gray hair fell across her face, eyes wide and watchful as if she’d been expecting something, but Willow’s mind was still stuck on Ellis. She kept wondering if he was already on his way back to his pack in the north.
The steps creaked softly beneath her feet, moss growing stubbornly between the cracks. By the time she reached the front door, her hand resting against the cool wood, it hit her. She hadn’t said anything to Ester. No hello, no small smile, nothing at all.
The memory of their awkward market exchange yesterday nudged at her. Hopefully, Ester wouldn’t think her silence had anything to do with that.
Ester had once admitted that she often felt invisible. Willow felt a small twist of guilt. She hadn’t meant to add to that feeling. She told herself she would make it up to Ester later.
Willow’s boots echoed on the uneven cobblestones as she walked.
A werewolf passed by, broad-shouldered and relaxed, carrying a crate under one arm like it weighed nothing. A witch argued with a shopkeeper over the quality of salt, magic flickering unconsciously from her fingertips. A vampire stood in the shade across the street.
Even here, in this mismatched city, people noticed vampires. Fairies zipped past him, wings flicking nervously. A witch muttered something under her breath and steered wide around him.
He wasn’t doing anything. Just standing there.
Willow felt heat rise in her chest. They all acted as if he might snap at any moment, as if simply existing was suspicious. She flexed her wings beneath her skin, irritation simmering.
He met her eyes and she offered him a small smile. For the briefest moment, he returned it, just the hint of a smile. 
Such sadness, such ache. She forced herself to keep moving.
She soon found herself at Mila’s door. She knocked, causing the wreath upon it to rock gently. 
Willow heard footsteps followed by the sound of Mila sliding open the eye hole. She unlatched the door to let Willow in.
“So you’ve heard,” Mila said, waving her inside.
“Yes,” Willow answered. She resisted the urge to ask what happened and settled for something safer. “How are you?”
Mila put her hands on her hips and leaned against her stone counter. “I loved him, Willow.”
As Willow searched for what to say, her eyes lingered on her friend. Mila had one of those faces you kept looking at without meaning to. She looked young at first glance, almost soft, with big eyes that carried a spark of mischief, even when tiredness dulled them. Her cheeks were hollow, her chin and jaw so slight they nearly disappeared, giving her a look that was a little strange and hard to place.
But the longer Willow watched her, the older she seemed. Not in her face, but in her mind, in the way she moved. Mila carried herself with an easy, knowing confidence, like someone who had lived more life than her years suggested. Her shoulder-length hair was always a bit wild, falling where it wanted, and somehow that only added to it. People noticed her, Willow included. Everyone seemed to agree she was beautiful, even if no one could quite say why.
“Do you think he’ll come back?” Willow asked. That was a dumb question. Ellis wasn’t the type to change his mind or admit he made a mistake. Chances were, none of them would ever see him again.
Mila didn’t answer right away. She stood at the counter of her narrow kitchen, crushing dried herbs into a stone mortar. The cottage was small and crooked, built like it had grown there instead of being constructed. Wooden beams arched overhead, shelves sagged under the weight of jars and bottles, and the windows were fogged with the evening chill. Candles lined the counters and windowsills, their flames steady and warm, casting soft gold light over everything. 
“I don’t think so,” Mila said. Her voice was eerily calm. 
Willow drifted a little closer. “Is there anything I can do?” 
Mila turned sharply. Her hair swung around her face, and her eyes were narrowed, almost frightening in their focus. “Turn back time,” she said. “So I could ask him to stay.”
The candles reacted before Willow could answer. Their flames tipped toward Mila all at once, like something in the room had shifted direction. Wax dripped unevenly down one side of the tallest candle, and the scent of crushed herbs thickened in the air.
Willow frowned. “Mila…”
But Mila was staring down at the mortar. “I loved him,” she said, her voice low but steady. “I would have given him anything.”
Something in the room felt… charged. The fine green powder in the bowl trembled slightly beneath the pestle, though Mila’s hands had gone still.
Willow stepped closer. The candlelight caught in her curls, turning them almost silver. “You can’t give someone everything,” she said gently. “That’s not how it works.”
Mila looked up at her then, and for a split second her expression shifted into something Willow couldn’t quite place.
“What if I could?” Mila asked.
The question hung there.
One candle flared brighter than the rest, its flame stretching tall. Another dimmed until the wick glowed red at its center. The shadows along the beams overhead warped around Mila making her the center point of the room.
Willow felt her own magic stir in response, instinctively reaching for Mila the way it always did when her friends were hurting. Usually it flowed easily, softening the edges of things.
This time it didn’t soften anything.
It didn’t bounce back, either.
It felt like it was being absorbed.
“Mila,” she said again, more firmly.
Mila blinked, as if waking up. The intensity drained from her face, leaving only exhaustion behind.
“I just wish someone would stay,” she murmured.
The candles slowly righted themselves. The shadows settled back into place. 
Willow swallowed the unease rising in her throat. “Someone will,” she said, hoping it was true.
Mila didn’t respond. She turned back to the counter and continued grinding, though there was nothing left to grind.
“If you wouldn’t mind,” she said, “I’d like to be alone.”
Willow nodded. When she let her magic reach out one last time, it didn’t slip away like before.
 Brax
November 24th - Evening
Brax sat at the bar of The Raven, staring at the rows of liquor bottles lined up behind the bar. They glittered under shifting lights that rippled across the ceiling in a restless kaleidoscope of color. Blues bled into pinks and golds into greens. The constant motion made the room feel unsteady. He wouldn’t need a drink to leave with a headache.
Music pulsed through the floorboards. His claustrophobia tightened when a wing brushed the back of his shoulder. Two fairies squeezed past him, leaving a faint shimmer of dust in the air.
He shot them a look. They didn’t apologize.
When he turned back around, he found himself face-to-face with Laurence.
Laurence was the owner of The Raven. He stood behind the bar, polishing a glass with a soft cloth. His silver hair was tied loosely at the nape of his neck, sleeves rolled to reveal forearms dusted faintly with glittering marks. His wings were tucked neatly behind him, translucent and veined with pale gold. Even working, he carried himself like someone hosting a private gathering in his own living room.
This place belonged to him. From what Brax had heard, Laurence had bought the building when it was nothing but a charred shell and rebuilt it himself from the bare bones up.
Laurence designed the place in such a way that everyone would feel like they belonged. There were tall-backed velvet chairs in dark corners for the vampires, ivy-infested coves carved into the walls for the fairies, tables with runes etched into them for the witches and near the back there was a sunken pit ringed with rough timber beams and iron hooks  for the werewolves. Each section of the room felt like it belonged to a different world. The effort showed, but so did Laurence’s need to be liked.
That subtle feeling of trying too hard and wanting approval from everyone made Brax’s skin crawl in ways he didn’t fully understand.
Brax rolled his shoulders, resisting the urge to leave. He didn’t like it here. 
Still, Willow would be more comfortable meeting him in this neutral space than at The Lair. The Lair was unmistakably vampire territory. Dim corridors, dark wood, lush seating…. A few wandering travelers drifted through from time to time, drawn by the heat of the hearth fires, but everyone knew who truly claimed it.
The Lair had better company, quieter corners, and fewer eyes.
But this would have to do.
A commotion drew his attention. At first, he thought it was just some obnoxious loser trying to get attention, but then he noticed the girl. She was shrinking into her chair, eyes wide. Something about her made his instincts flare.
Brax moved before he thought, stepping in front of the man with a predator’s precision. Their noses were almost touching. He could smell the sharp tang of the man’s alcohol mixing with the underlying scent of wet fur. Then he registered it fully. The man wasn’t just drunk. He was a werewolf.
He should’ve known better than to make a public scene with a werewolf. The ground between vampires and werewolves was thin and treacherous. If his father knew what he was about to do, he’d be shaking in his coffin. He could almost hear the old man shouting from the castle back East, reprimanding him for the rule he hadn’t even broken yet.
The bar fell quiet. Everyone was waiting to see who would strike first.
“Outside, if you must, boys,” Laurence added evenly. “Not in my house.”
Before Brax could respond, the door chimed, and another voice floated in.
“Brax?” a voice came from the door.
Shoot. Willow.
He tore his eyes away from the dog with all the willpower he could muster, forcing himself to back off. Every instinct screamed to stay, to dominate, to assert, but he had to keep his control, at least for now.
“That’s what I thought,” the werewolf huffed, his nostrils flaring.
Brax bit his tongue hard enough to taste phantom blood.
Willow fluttered over, concern etched into her face. She looked at Brax first, then the girl who was quickly gathering her things to leave.
“What happened here?” she asked, tucking one of her curls behind her ear, her voice soft but steady.
“Nothing,” Brax huffed. Plopping back down into his chair.”Did you talk to Mila? What did she say?”
“She just needs some time.” Willow answered. Her eyes lingered on the stool next to him. She decided ultimately on the next one down.
The tightness around Laurence’s shoulders eased. A faint smile touched his mouth. Without being asked, he reached for a tall glass.
“You said you weren’t coming today,” he said to Willow.
“I gotta keep you on your toes,” she replied, leaning her elbows onto the bar like she had done it a hundred times before. “It makes me mysterious.”
Laurence huffed a quiet laugh. “You are many things. Mysterious is not one of them.”
Their familiarity was effortless. Intimate in a way that had nothing to do with romance.
“You who…!” someone yelled from the other side of the bar, waving their hand aggressively. Brax would’ve been irritated by the rudeness, but Laurence didn’t mind. He smiled, bright and easy, and with a flutter of his wings moved over to take their order.
“We have to do something,” Brax said, looking irritated.
“We have to let her feel it. It’s what she needs,” Willow replied gently, eyes lingering on him in a way that made his chest tighten.
Brax sank beside her, scowling. He’d barely tolerated the stares before. Now they’d doubled now that he had a fairy sitting next to him. Perfect.

The thought of leaving had been growing quietly in the back of her mind for weeks.
Going home.
The idea was not exactly comforting. Her family would welcome her back, but they would also remind her of every decision they thought she had made incorrectly. They would tell her she was wasting her abilities, that a witch with her potential should not spend her days cooking meals for strangers.
Dani had always hated when they said things like that.
The worst part was that recently, she had started wondering if maybe they were right.
At home, she would have time.
Time to study, time to practice, and time to become something other than the person everyone relied on but rarely noticed.
“Are you going to help me finish cleaning, or am I doing that too?” Ester’s voice pulled her from her thoughts.
Dani turned slowly.
The frustration that had been sitting quietly inside her all day finally found somewhere to go. “I think the least you could do is tidy up the kitchen.”
Ester looked at her. She didn[t look offended or surprised which only made Dani angrier. “I mean it,” Dani continued. “I spent the entire day trying to keep everything running while you barely did anything.”
The words sounded harsher out loud than they had in her head.
Ester set the cloth down.
“Dani-”
“I’m tired of feeling like if I stop doing things, everything falls apart.” She had not meant to raise her voice, but once she started, she couldn’t seem to stop. “I’m tired of being the one who notices everything. I’m tired of working myself to exhaustion and acting like it doesn’t bother me.”
The kitchen fell silent.
Dani looked away.
For a moment, Ester said nothing. Then she looked toward the hallway. “I’m going to go check on Willow.”
Dani stared at her.
A strange emptiness replaced the anger.
She had wanted Ester to understand or to say something. Anything really.
Instead, Ester simply smoothed her hair back, straightened her shoulders, and walked out of the room.
Dani remained standing in the middle of the kitchen long after Ester had disappeared.
The anger was gone now and only regret remained.
She looked around one more time.
Near the ceiling, a thin web stretched between two wooden beams.
She used to clean those.
She sighed again, this time softer. She should appologize. With that thought, she left the kitchen. Dani found Ester at the end of the hallway, standing outside Willow’s room.
For a moment, she only watched her.
There was something strangely painful about seeing Ester there, so calm and familiar after the sharpness of the argument. The same person who had spent years moving through the world with that quiet patience of hers, as if nothing could truly surprise her anymore.
“Ester,” Dani said quietly. “I’m sorry. I didn’t mean what I said back there. I’ve just been overwhelmed.”
But Ester didn’t answer.
Dani frowned.
Ester wasn’t ignoring her. She wasn’t angry.
She was listening.
Her eyes had moved past Dani, fixed on something behind her.
The change in her expression was small. Most people wouldn’t have noticed it. A slight tightening around her eyes. The way her shoulders went still.
But Dani knew Ester.
And Ester was afraid.
The realization settled over Dani slowly.
That was what scared her most.
They both knew what existed in the dark. Werewolves weren’t myths or stories told to frighten children. They were real. They had hunted, killed, and haunted their world for longer than either of them wanted to think about.
But Ester had never been afraid of them.
Not really.
Ester had survived things Dani only knew through history books and old stories. She had lived through wars, watched generations disappear, carried memories that no one else could understand.
She was the person who stayed calm when everyone else broke.
So when Ester whispered, “Dani… slowly walk toward me,” the fear in her voice made Dani’s blood run cold.
“What is it?”
“Don’t turn around,” Ester said.
Dani obeyed. She took one reluctant step, then another. 
Every instinct told her to look, but Ester’s eyes stayed locked over Dani’s shoulder in such a way that made her scared to.
Then Dani heard it.
A heavy breath behind her.
The sound of claws dragging lightly across the floor made Ester’s face change.
Dani had never seen her look like that.
Dani finally turned her head, and saw it.
A werewolf stood only a few feet away, its massive frame almost too large for the narrow hallway it occupied, its shoulders nearly brushing the walls as it lowered itself toward the ground with the slow, controlled movement of something that knew exactly how much power it held. Dark fur covered its body unevenly, stretched across muscle that shifted beneath its skin with every breath, while its claws pressed into the old wooden floor hard enough to leave deep marks behind.
Dani smelled it before she was even able to fully understand what she was looking at.
Blood, dirt, and something rotten filled the air, something ancient and animalistic that made every instinct inside her scream that she was standing in front of something she was never supposed to survive.
The creature tilted its head, watching them.
For one terrifying moment, Dani forgot how to breathe.
She knew what she was supposed to do.
She had magic.
She had spent years learning how to use it, years trying to understand the power inside her, and yet when the moment finally came, when someone she cared about needed her to step forward, her body refused to listen. The magic was there, waiting beneath her skin, but fear wrapped around her tighter than any spell ever could.
Ester moved before Dani could.
She stepped between them without hesitation, placing herself directly in the path of the creature.
The hallway shook as the werewolf charged.
Ester did not run. Her eyes narrowed, her fangs lowered, and she met the creature head-on.
The impact was violent enough to crack the old wooden floor beneath them.
For a moment, the two of them were completely still, locked against each other as strength fought against strength. The werewolf was larger, heavier, and driven by a kind of rage that made it impossible to predict. Ester was faster. She moved with the precision of a predator, twisting out of its grasp and striking before it could recover.
Her claws cut through its fur.
Her teeth sank into its shoulder.
The werewolf tore itself away from her, stumbling back only a few steps before turning toward her again.
Ester wiped the blood from her mouth and stared at it.
The werewolf attacked again, and this time Ester barely had enough time to react. She blocked the first strike, but the force behind it sent her crashing into the wall hard enough to splinter the wood behind her.
“Ester!”
Dani’s voice broke through the silence.
Ester pushed herself away from the wall and stood again, her breathing was heavier now.
Dani wanted to help.
She wanted to prove that she was more than someone who needed to be saved.
But her body refused to listen. The same fear that had always trapped her held her in place, and she hated herself for it.
The werewolf lunged.
Ester moved with it, intercepting the attack before it could reach Dani. She drove her claws into the creature’s side and held on, using every ounce of strength she had left. The werewolf roared and grabbed her, but Ester refused to let go.
She fought harder and for a moment, it looked like she might actually win.
Then the werewolf overpowered her.
Its grip tightened, and Ester’s expression changed as she realized she could not break free.
Dani felt panic take over.
“No, no, no…”
The werewolf pulled her away from the ground and slammed her down with enough force to shake the hallway. Ester tried to move, tried to fight back, but the creature was already on top of her.
Dani finally forced herself forward.
She ran.
But she was too late.
The attack happened so quickly that her mind struggled to process it. The werewolf’s claws tore through Ester’s chest, slicing through her skin and pulling out her organs. [1.1]
It grabbed her heart, slow and deliberate and snarled before tearing into it with it’s teeth.
The only true way to kill a vampire was to get to it’s heart.
Dani felt as though the wind had been knocked out of her. She stumbled backwards and hurried along the wall until until she made it to the stairway. She flew out into the streets of Stillwater crying and screaming, “Help! Werewolf!”
